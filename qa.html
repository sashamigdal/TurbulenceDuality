Dr. Migdal’s approach starts with the Hopf equation—a functional differential equation that governs the evolution of the characteristic functional (or generating functional) of the velocity field in turbulence. He takes multiple derivatives (in effect, area derivatives) of the Hopf functional to extract vorticity correlation functions. This process recasts the nonlinear Navier–Stokes problem into a linear problem in loop space, leading to the momentum loop equation. This reformulation is key to establishing the duality between fluid dynamics and quantum mechanics in loop space.

The Euler ensemble is a fixed point solution of the momentum loop equation derived from the Hopf functional. Rather than working directly in physical space, the turbulence problem is recast in loop space, where the Euler ensemble is constructed as the limit (N → ∞) of a polygonal approximation of a closed loop. Its vertices are determined by a set of rotation parameters and angular increments that enforce periodicity and symmetry. Remarkably, this construction is equivalent to a random walk on regular star polygons and can be interpreted as a fermionic string theory. This duality underpins an exact analytic solution for decaying turbulence.

Unlike the classical K41 scaling laws, which predict a single universal scaling exponent (for example, the second moment of velocity scaling as Δv² ~ r^(2/3)), the Euler ensemble theory introduces logarithmic corrections along with an infinite discrete spectrum of anomalous scaling dimensions. This means that the energy spectrum and other statistical measures are described by a universal function of log k rather than a simple power law. This modification accounts for the deviations observed in decaying turbulence, where log–log plots exhibit curvature that a pure power law cannot capture.

Q: What is turbulence duality?

A: Turbulence duality reinterprets hydrodynamic turbulence as a solvable problem in string theory, particularly using regular star polygons as the target space.
